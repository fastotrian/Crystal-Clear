Water Pollution

Water pollution occurs when harmful substances often chemicals or microorganisms—contaminate a stream, river, lake, ocean, aquifer, or other body of water, degrading water quality and rendering it toxic to humans or the environment Water pollution can have severe consequences for both human health and the environment. Contaminated water sources can lead to the spread of diseases, disrupt aquatic ecosystems, and harm wildlife. Effects

Contaminated water and poor sanitation are linked to transmission of diseases such as cholera, diarrhoea, dysentery, hepatitis A, typhoid and polio. Absent, inadequate, or inappropriately managed water and sanitation services expose individuals to preventable health risks. This is particularly the case in health care facilities where both patients and staff are placed at additional risk of infection and disease when water, sanitation and hygiene services are lacking.

Solution

The water pollution problem can be addressed through ten key solutions. First, wastewater treatment is essential to reduce pollutants entering water bodies. Reducing plastic waste and conserving water are also critical steps to minimize pollution. The use of water-saving toilets and proper septic tank maintenance can further decrease water contamination. It's important to avoid using toilets as garbage bins, as this prevents unnecessary waste from entering the water system. Rainwater management and promoting green agriculture and wetlands help naturally filter pollutants. Additionally, denitrification processes can reduce harmful nitrogen compounds, and ozone wastewater treatment can effectively disinfect and purify water.
